2018: The Year of the the Blockchain

2017 was an unprecendented year in crypto. Bitcoin went from around £750 in January to the heady heights of £14,500 in December.

 

From around May onwards the market saw a huge inflow of new people. I watched Facebook pages such as Cryptocurrency Collectors Club grew from a couple of thousand members to over 30k within a very short period of time (now the largest page on Facebook now boasts over 100k members). Bitcoin was the tide that many projects rose on and the market became a beacon for investors from across the board.

 

The Christmas season saw a repeat performance of previous years with big sell offs and now at the beginning of 2018 all eyes are on the market to see if this year will improve on last or pave the way for a drawn out bear market.

 

I think that 2018 will eclipse 2017 beyond all recognition and here’s why.

 

The SEC and CTFC Testimonial February 6th 2018

 

The SEC (Securities Exchange Commission) and the CTFC (Commodity Futures Trading Commission) are two regulatory bodies in America. Regulation has been a primary source for FUD (fear, uncertainty and doubt) in the market and has historically seen big sell offs.

 

Here’s what came out of a recent hearing of testimonials from Jay Clayton and J. Christopher Giancarlo.

 

Technology:

 

‘It’s also important to note that the technology innovation and ideas underlying these markets present significant positive potential. These aspects underpinning virtual currencies have the ability to transform for investors the composition of and the ability to access the financial landscape.Thus changing and modernising capital formation and transfer of risk. Technology is forward looking and we look to our regulators to continue carrying out their mandates including investor protection as market evolve.’

 

Senator Mike Crapo opening the February 6th 2018 Senate Testimony on cryptocurrencies

 

‘To be clear, I am very optimistic that developments in financial technology will help facilitate capital formation, providing promising investment opportunities for institutional and Main Street investors alike. From a financial regulatory perspective, these developments may enable us to better monitor transactions, holdings and obligations (including credit exposures) and other activities and characteristics of our markets, thereby facilitating our regulatory mission, including, importantly, investor protection.’

 

Jay Clayton, Chairman SEC

 

‘I have spoken publicly about the potential benefits of the technology underlying Bitcoin, namely Blockchain or distributed ledger technology (DLT). Distributed ledgers – in various open system or private network applications – have the potential to enhance economic efficiency, mitigate centralized systemic risk, defend against fraudulent activity and improve data quality and governance.

 

DLT is likely to have a broad and lasting impact on global financial markets in payments, banking, securities settlement, title recording, cyber security and trade reporting and analysis. When tied to virtual currencies, this technology aims to serve as a new store of value, facilitate secure payments, enable asset transfers, and power new applications.

 

Additionally, DLT will likely develop hand-in-hand with new “smart” contracts that can value themselves in real-time, report themselves to data repositories, automatically calculate and perform margin payments and even terminate themselves in the event of counterparty default.

 

DLT may enable financial market participants to manage the significant operational, transactional and capital complexities brought about by the many mandates, regulations and capital requirements promulgated by regulators here and abroad in the wake of the financial crisis. In fact, one study estimates that DLT could eventually allow financial institutions to save as much as $20 billion in infrastructure and operational costs each year. Another study reportedly estimates that blockchain could cut trading settlement costs by a third, or $16 billion a year, and cut capital requirements by $120 billion. Moving from systems-of-record at the level of a firm to an authoritative system-of-record at the level of a market is an enormous opportunity to improve existing market infrastructure.

 

Outside of the financial services industry, many use cases for DLT are being posited from international trade to charitable endeavors and social services. International agricultural commodities merchant, Louis Dreyfus, and a group of financing banks have just completed the first agricultural deal using distributed ledger technology for the sale of 60,000 tons of US soybeans to China. Other DLT use cases include: legal records management, inventory control and logistics, charitable donation tracking and confirmation; voting security and human refugee identification and relocation.

 

Yet, while DLT promises enormous benefits to commercial firms and charities, it also promises assistance to financial market regulators in meeting their mission to oversee healthy markets and mitigate financial risk. What a difference it would have made on the eve of the financial crisis in 2008 if regulators had access to the real-time trading ledgers of large Wall Street banks, rather than trying to assemble piecemeal data to recreate complex, individual trading portfolios. I have previously speculated32 that, if regulators in 2008 could have viewed a real-time distributed ledger (or a series of aggregated ledgers across asset classes) and, perhaps, been able to utilize modern cognitive computing capabilities, they may have been able to recognize anomalies in market-wide trading activity and diverging counterparty exposures indicating heightened risk of bank failure. Such transparency may not, by itself, have saved Lehman Brothers from bankruptcy, but it certainly would have allowed for far prompter, betterinformed, and more calibrated regulatory intervention instead of the disorganized response that unfortunately ensued.’

 

J. Christopher Giancarlo Chairman CTFC

 

Market:

 

‘Perspective is critically important. As of the morning of February 5, the total value of all outstanding Bitcoin was about $130 billion based on a Bitcoin price of $7,700. The Bitcoin “market capitalization” is less than the stock market capitalization of a single “large cap” business, such as McDonalds (around $130 billion). The total value of all outstanding virtual currencies was about $365 billion. Because virtual currencies like Bitcoin are sometimes considered to be comparable to gold as an investment vehicle, it is important to recognize that the total value of all the gold in the world is estimated by the World Gold Council to be about $8 trillion which continues to dwarf the virtual currency market size. Clearly, the column inches of press attention to virtual currency far surpass its size and magnitude in today’s global economy.’

 

J. Christopher Giancarlo Chairman CTFC

TL;DR? After researching blockchain America’s two most prominent regulatory bodies have found that the tech is profoundly innovative with the potential to reshape global business and finance positively and the market is still in its infancy and relatively small.

 

If this came from a Twitter account called ‘CryptoCool’ you would rightfully take it with a pinch of salt, however, this comes from representatives of the SEC and the CTFC.

 

We are at the beginning of a market for a technology which has the potential to disrupt and reshape business and finance across the board.

 

They even note that blockchain can be utilised to improve financial regulation. They’re sold because it’ll make their job easier!

 

I personally can’t think of anything more bullish.

 

However, as with all emerging markets there are real risks. There are a lot of fraudulent practices capitalising on lack of regulation and general naivety in the market. Crypto has largely evolved virally online so ‘retail’ or ‘main street’ investors are coming in and are often easy targets for scams.

 

Some kind of regulation is absolutely necessary not only to protect new investors but also to protect the reputation of this new technology and its innovation.

 

So how can you sift through the 1000’s of projects out there and have any assurance as to who is legitimate and who isn’t?

 

 

Enter Stage Enterprise

 

The lure of blockchain for enterprise is strong with or without regulation.

 

The above stats on how much money blockchain can save institutions gives a very immediate reason as to why enterprises are jumping in. Businesses have to be seen to be competitive. Shareholders won’t be happy if their investment is getting left behind in the stone-age.

 

Historically we’ve seen blockchain start ups partnering with big brands and benefiting from the legitimacy of association and consequent flood of money that’ll flow into the project (Ethereum’s partnership with Microsoft early on was one of the reasons I got in just before Homestead was launched). Now we’re seeing companies announcing their involvement with blockchain projects with a similar fervour so as to prove their legitimacy in keeping up with new technology.

 

Industries across the board are trialling or even implementing blockchain. Retail, automotive, aviation, supply chain, TIC, auditing, government, finance, the list goes on. Even BNSF, the railway company owned by huge blockchain sceptic Warren Buffet, is now signed up to the Blockchain in Transport Alliance (https://uk.reuters.com/article/uk-berkshire-bnsf-blockchain/buffetts-bnsf-railroad-eyes-blockchain-for-shipping-freight-idUKKBN1FP2L3).

 

So enterprise adoption can be a great starting point for a seeing if a project is legitimate or not.

 

There are some pretty important questions new investors should ask when they’re researching a project that’s caught their eye. Does the project solve a real world problem? Do they have a team that can deliver? Have they already secured partnerships that can implement their solution?

 

For example: if you were comparing two projects, would you feel more comfortable putting money into a one that has a proven working partnership with one of the ‘big four’ auditing companies well known for their diligence and analysis of businesses worldwide; or a project with an anonymous team and a white paper full of grand ideas but no way of delivering them?

 

There are moves for the market to be self regulating (https://www.coindesk.com/cftc-official-backs-winklevoss-brothers-crypto-self-regulation-bid/) and as an example of basic free market economics, enterprise adoption can pave the way for a stronger market. Vapour-ware projects without partnerships or the teams to realise their vision will fall away, leaving projects with strong fundamentals serving the needs of enterprise.

 

No doubt in the future we’ll see the market regulated by blockchain itself.

 

 

But surely large companies coming into the market will go for private blockchain companies or simply make their own and lock out retail investors?

 

In terms of making their own private blockchain it’s really not so easy plus why make your own when you can use a project on the market that’s already tried and tested project and offering the exact service you need. The resources it would take to get up to speed with already active projects would be costly and potentially put the company behind the curve. It’s more efficient to outsource.

 

In terms of private vs public there are instances of big corporate entities trialling private blockchain (such as Maersk using Guardtime KSI), however, the argument for public blockchains over private is a very easy one. Public blockchains mitigate risk by tokenising data. If a company’s data is monopolised by one centralized intermediary, it’s handing over a lot of control, responsibility and ultimately power to one entity.

 

The public tokenisation of data that tracks trillions of fiat value transfers will be essential for the scale needed for enterprise adoption. The prospect of tokenising the goods valued in fiat then leads to the prospect of the tokenisation of fiat currencies which takes us from enterprise adoption to something far more compelling.

 

 

The Rise of the Blockchain State

 

Above we looked into the eye of the FUD storm that is regulation and seen that at its heart, sentiment is incredibly positive. Now lets delve into another faithful source of FUD within the market: China.

China has a huge influence over the market. In June 2017 it was estimated that around 70% of all Bitcoin mining happened in China. In January 2017 it was estimated that 98% of crypto trading volume came from China.

The smallest of noises from China’s government has historically sent shockwaves through the space. This culminated to a point in September 2017 when China ‘banned Bitcoin’. This was the headline that was leapt on by media outlets across the board. Bitcoin was dead. Crypto was scam. The Wolf of Wall Street guy was right all along!

However, on closer inspection, like with the SEC and CTFC hearing, the Chinese government is clearly pro-blockchain. There was speculation that the timing of the announcements coincided brilliantly with the lead up to China’s ‘elections’ (19th National Congress of the Communist Party of China) and played into the narrative of a strong governing hand over the economic welfare of the country. As with the SEC and CTFC they want proper regulation and controls in place, however, the key word in this case is ‘control’.

We are seeing very clear signs that China are set to become a world leader in applying blockchain tech. Recently The People’s Daily (the official newspaper of the Chinese Communist Party) printed a feature endorsing blockchain.

We should plow deep into technology in the hope that through technological superiority we will be invincible in the future international blockchain competition, but If you are anxious to build a castle on a not-so-strong foundation, it is quite possible that you will lose.

Dou Jiali. Researcher at Peking University Guanghua Financial Technology Laboratory Researcher. The People’s Daily

The same drive that is luring enterprise onto the blockchain also exists for nation states and I believe that this drive is down to what has rapidly become recognised as ‘new oil’ and this is big data.

Tokenisation:

China is already close to being a cashless society. QR payments are increasingly popular but PboC are concerned about security risks and unfair market competition so there’s been much speculation about a state run digital currency in the pipelines. However, this is a pretty simple understanding of China’s approach to blockchain. Blockchain offers many possibilities beyond currency.

‘The foundation of this high-value future is the concept of tokenization: representing a company's products and services as digital tokens on a blockchain, not merely as items of information, but as carriers of value.

Such digital tokens can stand for anything from pharmaceuticals to phones to music. Whatever they are, they should have assigned ownership and value. If you have a pallet of 1,000 mobile phones, each worth $1,000, that's on a ship in transit, the blockchain should represent that with 1,000 small tokens with a collective value of $1 million.

When products are made, delivered or sold, value in the blockchain can change hands: 1,000 phone tokens for $1 million in U.S. dollar tokens. Not just bitcoin or ether, but U.S. dollar. Or euros or yen for that matter.

Set aside the debate about the value of traditional fiat vs. cryptocurrencies for the long run. The simple fact is that enterprise CFOs want to be paid the same currencies in which they have expenses and long-term liabilities.

That means tokenizing traditional fiat currency and doing so in the same blockchain as the products and services are tokenized.

Paul Brody principal and global innovation leader for blockchain technology at Ernst & Young

So here we see the potential for a far more all encompassing application of blockchain.

Smart Cities

In December 2017 it was announced that Gui’an was to become a new Free Trade Zone. It’s earmarked to become one of the most important national level data centre clusters in China and a model that is intended to be rolled out on a national level.

This New Area will be run on a public blockchain.

Business registration related documents, such as a business certificates, bank account certificates, tax registrations, organization codes, foreign trade registrations, audit reports, etc are already stored on the blockchain by the The Administrative Examination and Approval Bureau of Gui’an New Area. Administration, agriculture, retail, transport, energy trading and much more are all set to be run on the blockchain as well. Gui’an will also introduce a physical goods exchange for wine (the provenance of which is verified by blockchain) run on the blockchain, providing a test bed for a future exchange for a range of products. Imagine an Alibaba for authentic goods.

In Gui’an we are seeing the realisation of value transfer on the blockchain with tokens being the carriers of that value. For anyone who’s seen the rapid rise of cities like Shenzhen, the prospect of what is about to be realised in Gui’an is very compelling.



As with the SEC and CTFC hearing we see that at the heart of the FUD storm of ‘China banning Bitcoin’ is the very clear prospect that China realises that adopting blockchain technology first will give them the competitive advantage internationally.



TL;DR: SEC, CTFC, enterprise and China are fomo’ing into blockchain
